Kurt Vonnegut Jr.(November 11, 1922 – April 11, 2007)

Kurt Vonnegut was an American writer known for his darkly humorous and satirical novels, which often dealt with the absurdity and futility of modern life. His unique writing style, which blended science fiction, humor, and social commentary, has made him a beloved and enduring figure in American literature.

Kurt Vonnegut was born on November 11, 1922, in Indianapolis, Indiana. He was the youngest of three children in a wealthy family. After attending Cornell University, he enlisted in the U.S. Army and served in World War II as an infantryman, during which he was captured and held as a prisoner of war in Germany.

After the war, he worked as a journalist for several years before beginning his career as a fiction writer in the 1950s.

His first novel, "Player Piano," was published in 1952.

His most famous works include:

  • "Slaughterhouse-Five",
  • "Cat's Cradle",
  • "Breakfast of Champions".

Vonnegut was also a social and political activist who frequently spoke out against war and nuclear weapons. He died on April 11, 2007, in New York City, at the age of 84. Today, he is widely regarded as one of the most important American writers of the 20th century.
